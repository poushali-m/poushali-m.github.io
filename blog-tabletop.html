What a Tabletop Security Exercise Actually Looks Like

By Poushali Majumder  ·  April 2026  ·  6 min read

Most organisations have an incident response plan sitting in a folder somewhere. Some have even had it reviewed by a consultant. But when was the last time anyone actually tested it?

A tabletop security exercise is one of the most cost-effective ways to find out whether your team is truly prepared — before a real incident forces you to find out the hard way.

What is a tabletop exercise?

A tabletop exercise is a structured discussion-based simulation of a security incident. Unlike a full live-fire drill, there's no actual system compromise, no downtime, and no risk to your production environment. Instead, a facilitator walks participants through a realistic attack scenario, and the team works through how they would respond at each stage.

Think of it as a rehearsal. The goal isn't to pass or fail — it's to identify gaps, clarify responsibilities, and build muscle memory so that when something real happens, your team isn't making it up as they go.

The best time to discover that your incident response plan has gaps is during a tabletop exercise — not during an actual ransomware attack at 2am on a Friday.

Who should be in the room?

This is where many organisations get it wrong — they treat tabletop exercises as purely an IT event. In reality, a security incident affects the whole business, and the most valuable exercises involve representatives from multiple departments:

  • IT and Security — for technical response decisions
  • Legal and Compliance — for regulatory notification requirements and liability considerations
  • Communications / PR — for managing internal and external messaging
  • HR — particularly relevant for insider threat scenarios
  • Senior Management / CEO — because major incidents require executive decision-making
  • Finance — for understanding the business impact of downtime or ransom demands

The facilitator — usually a security professional — guides the discussion, injects new information as the scenario evolves, and asks probing questions to test assumptions.

What does a scenario look like?

Scenarios are designed to be realistic and relevant to the organisation's specific threat landscape. Here's an example of how one might unfold:

Example Scenario — Phishing & Ransomware

Monday 09:15 — An employee in the finance team reports that their computer is displaying an unusual message and they can no longer access their files. Within the next 20 minutes, three more employees in the same department report the same issue. The IT helpdesk notices unusual outbound network traffic...

From this starting point, the facilitator will pose questions to the group:

  • Who is the first person to be notified, and through what channel?
  • At what point do you isolate affected systems — and who makes that call?
  • Do you have a clean backup, and how long would restoration take?
  • Are you required to notify the ICO within 72 hours under GDPR?
  • What do you tell staff? What do you tell clients?
  • Who has the authority to decide whether to pay a ransom?

The discomfort of not having clear answers to these questions in a meeting room is far preferable to not having them during a live incident.

What good looks like

A well-run tabletop exercise will typically:

  • Run for 2–4 hours, with a structured scenario and defined inject points
  • Include a facilitator who challenges assumptions and introduces complications
  • Surface genuine gaps — undocumented escalation paths, unclear ownership, missing contact lists
  • Produce a written after-action report with specific recommendations
  • Be followed up — the recommendations need owners and deadlines, not just a report that gets filed away

Common findings from tabletop exercises

Having facilitated tabletop exercises with clients, the most common findings tend to fall into a few recurring patterns:

  • Unclear ownership — nobody is sure who is the incident lead when the CISO is unavailable
  • Communication breakdowns — the incident response plan assumes email communication, which is unavailable during a major breach
  • Regulatory blind spots — teams are unsure of their GDPR notification obligations or the 72-hour reporting window
  • Backup assumptions — organisations assume their backups are clean and restorable but have never actually tested a full restore
  • No external contact list — key vendor contacts, legal counsel, and cyber insurance details are not readily accessible offline

How often should you run one?

At minimum, once a year — and after any significant change to your environment, such as a major cloud migration, acquisition, or shift to remote working. Many organisations that take security seriously run them twice a year, alternating between different scenario types (ransomware, insider threat, supply chain compromise, data breach, etc.).

The bottom line

A tabletop exercise won't make your organisation immune to attack. What it will do is ensure that when something happens — and in today's threat landscape, it's a question of when, not if — your team has a shared understanding of their roles, your plan has been stress-tested, and the gaps have been identified and addressed before they matter.

The cost of a two-hour meeting is negligible compared to the cost of a poorly managed incident.
