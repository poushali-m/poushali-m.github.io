What a Tabletop Security Exercise Actually Looks Like

By Poushali Majumder  ·  April 2026  ·  6 min read

Most organisations have an incident response plan sitting in a folder somewhere. Some have even had it reviewed by a consultant. But when was the last time anyone actually tested it?

A tabletop security exercise is one of the most cost-effective ways to find out whether your team is truly prepared — before a real incident forces you to find out the hard way.

What is a tabletop exercise?

A tabletop exercise is a structured discussion-based simulation of a security incident. Unlike a full live-fire drill, there's no actual system compromise, no downtime, and no risk to your production environment. Instead, a facilitator walks participants through a realistic attack scenario, and the team works through how they would respond at each stage.

Think of it as a rehearsal. The goal isn't to pass or fail — it's to identify gaps, clarify responsibilities, and build muscle memory so that when something real happens, your team isn't making it up as they go.

The best time to discover that your incident response plan has gaps is during a tabletop exercise — not during an actual ransomware attack at 2am on a Friday.

Who should be in the room?

This is where many organisations get it wrong — they treat tabletop exercises as purely an IT event. A security incident affects the whole business:

💻

IT & Security

Technical response decisions and system knowledge

⚖️

Legal & Compliance

Regulatory notification and liability considerations

📢

Communications

Internal and external messaging during an incident

👥

HR

Essential for insider threat and employee-related scenarios

🏢

Senior Management

Major incidents require executive decision-making authority

💰

Finance

Business impact of downtime, ransom demands, and recovery costs

What does a scenario look like?

Scenarios are designed to be realistic and relevant to the organisation's specific threat landscape. Here's how one might unfold in real time:

Example Scenario — Phishing & Ransomware

Monday 09:15 — An employee in the finance team reports that their computer is displaying an unusual message and they can no longer access their files. Within 20 minutes, three more colleagues report the same issue. The IT helpdesk notices unusual outbound network traffic...

The facilitator then injects new information at key decision points to test how the team responds under pressure:

09:20

Initial Detection

Who is the first person notified, and through what channel?

09:45

Containment Decision

At what point do you isolate affected systems — and who has the authority to make that call?

10:30

Regulatory Obligation

Are you required to notify the ICO within 72 hours? Who makes that determination?

11:00

Communication

What do you tell staff? What do you tell clients? Who approves external statements?

11:30

Recovery Decision

Do you have a clean backup? How long would full restoration take? Is paying a ransom on the table?

Common findings

Having facilitated tabletop exercises with clients, certain gaps appear again and again:

Unclear Ownership

Nobody is sure who is the incident lead when the CISO is unavailable

Communication Breakdown

The IR plan assumes email — which is unavailable during a major breach

Regulatory Blind Spots

Teams unsure of GDPR notification obligations or the 72-hour reporting window

Untested Backups

Organisations assume backups are clean and restorable but have never tested a full restore

Missing Contact List

Key vendor contacts, legal counsel, and cyber insurance details not accessible offline

No Escalation Path

Clear decision-making authority for critical choices like paying a ransom is undefined

How often should you run one?

At minimum, once a year — and after any significant change to your environment such as a major cloud migration, acquisition, or shift to remote working. Many organisations run them twice a year, alternating between different scenario types: ransomware, insider threat, supply chain compromise, data breach.

The bottom line

A tabletop exercise won't make your organisation immune to attack. What it will do is ensure that when something happens — and in today's threat landscape, it's a question of when, not if — your team has a shared understanding of their roles, your plan has been stress-tested, and the gaps have been addressed before they matter.

The cost of a two-hour meeting is negligible compared to the cost of a poorly managed incident.
